# Audience-Specific Messaging

Pearl serves four primary audiences. Each requires distinct messaging, timing, and tone. Content must be evaluated against the correct audience profile. Buyers are the foundational audience — "Win the buyer. Everything else follows."

## Buyers

**Core position:** Pearl is the buyer's guide to understanding a home.

**Three needs:** Understand (framework, vocabulary), Compare (same five dimensions across homes), Predict (translate performance into lived outcomes)

**Timing sequence:** Getting serious about a home → before the first visit → before the offer → before the inspection → before commitment hardens. "Performance information delivered early is a guide. The same information delivered late is a disruption."

**Key distinction:** First-time buyers fear the unknown. Repeat buyers fear the known (they've been burned before). Both need the framework.

**Guardrails:**
- Do not lead with cost savings — ranked last as purchase driver. Lead with safety, comfort, confidence.
- Do not position as inspection substitute
- Do not imply unclaimed SCORE is less reliable
- Do not overwhelm — one score, five pillars, clear questions
- Buyers fear unexpected capital events ($60K roof), not monthly utility differences

**Reason to believe:** 80%+ of buyers want specific performance information. 79% would use it to guide inspector questions. 90% say safety factors influence their purchase decision.

## Homeowners

**Core position:** Pearl is a mirror — showing homeowners how their home already appears in the public domain.

**Four needs:** Visibility (see public profile for first time), Context (relative to comparable homes), Agency (correct, complete, control the narrative), Readiness (enter transaction with story already told)

**Timing sequence:** General curiosity → considering a sale → preparing to list (before agent is signed). Pearl's direct engagement window closes once a listing agent is signed — decision-making authority transfers.

**Key message:** "The data that defines how the market sees your home is already out there. Pearl shows it to you."

**Guardrails:**
- Do not frame as surveillance or intrusion
- Do not imply typical/low score is a problem Pearl created
- Do not promise claiming increases sale price
- Do not pressure to claim — invitation, not threat
- "Claim your home to complete your home's story" — never "to fix your score"
- A typical score is good news — it means no significant performance concerns found

## Buy-Side Agents

**Core position:** Pearl keeps performance conversations on rails — organizing buyer anxiety into structured questions rather than scattered panic.

**What they need:** Process leverage (not more opinions or liability), focused and proportional questions, reduced late-stage surprises, something shareable without risk

**Timing sequence:** Before first serious conversation about a specific home → before the visit → before the offer → before the inspection → after inspection (interpret findings in proportion)

**Key message:** "Pearl addresses the invisible part of the transaction — the part that creates the most anxiety."

**Guardrails:**
- Do not position as negotiation tool
- Do not imply Pearl replaces agent judgment
- Do not introduce as additional complication — Pearl is a simplifier
- Do not position as inspection substitute

## Listing Agents

**Core position:** Pearl puts performance conversations on rails before they become transaction risk.

**Non-negotiable principle:** Pearl must make listing agents feel SAFER, not more exposed. They are the veto holders for market adoption.

**What they need:** Framework that sets expectations without diagnosing, protection from misstatements, earlier surfacing of performance topics, practice differentiator

**Timing sequence:** Listing preparation (guide seller to claim before listing) → active listing (buyers arrive oriented) → offer stage (shared ground truth) → pre-inspection (inspection confirms, doesn't introduce performance as new subject)

**Key message:** "The listing agent who introduces Pearl is not surfacing new problems. They are ensuring existing information is presented in the most organized, neutral, professionally managed way possible."

**Named objections with approved responses:**
1. "This score makes the house look bad" → Typical = no significant concerns. Withholding is riskier than presenting.
2. "Pearl could give buyers negotiation leverage" → Information already exists in public records. Organized early = less deal risk.
3. "This adds another step" → Pearl removes steps — the ones it removes happen late (renegotiations, deal collapses).

**Guardrails:**
- Do not position as listing price tool
- Do not imply Pearl creates disclosure obligations
- Do not use defect/flaw/issue/problem language
- Do not attack other tools or services
- Do not imply Pearl competes with inspections, Zillow, or MLS
- A typical score is a transparency advantage
